CASH WAQF SUKUK FOR POVERTY ALLEVIATION

Cash Waqf Sukuk (CWS) is an innovative financing mechanism that aims to mobilize resources through the issuance of sukuk (Sharia-compliant bonds). The funds generated from the Sukuk will be invested, and the proceeds will be transferred to the Islamic Solidarity Fund for Development (ISFD), the poverty alleviation arm of the Islamic Development Bank (IsDB). A unique feature of CWS is that profit generated from the investment of these Sukuk is directed to poverty alleviation programs rather than the Sukuk holders – (henceforth “benefactors”). Subscription to CWS is not restricted to Muslims only, it is open to all partners interested in committing part of their resources for poverty alleviation purposes.

The Sharia Board of the IsDB cleared the CWS, concept, and the ISFD Board has approved the issuance of up to US$500 MILLION of Sukuk, with an expected initial issuance of US$200 million in 2020".The CWS's specificity is that it targets both high net worth individuals and members of the public who can subscribe with as little as US$100. The CWS has a 10-year retention period before the principal amount is paid back to the Sukuk holders. CWS are "social instruments" whose sole purpose is to seek out interested philanthropists who would provide Waqf (endowment) in form of cash. Upon maturity of the Sukuk, the benefactors will get back their initial cash contributions.

OUT OF SCHOOL CHILDREN AND CASH WAQF SUKUK

There are at least 28 million Out of School Children in the Member Countries of the Organisation of Islamic Cooperation (OIC). The proceeds realized from Cash Waqf Sukuk will be directed to fund the Out of School Children Program in OIC Countries. IsDB/ISFD has signed a declaration with other development partners such as Save the Children, UK, Global Partnership for Education, Spark, International Federation of Red Cross and Red Crescent, Education Can not Wait, Light for the World, as well as the UN Special Envoy on Education and former British Prime Minister, Rt. Hon. Gordon Brown, to enroll all out-of-school children and youth in the OIC Countries back to school by 2030. This would contribute greatly to achieving the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs), especially SDG-4 on quality education and lifelong learning for all.

Takaful (insurance) guarantee of the Sukuk repayment

20% of annual income to be kept in a Reserve Account

Any capital appreciation after exiting from investments may be credited to the Reserve Account

Cash Waqf Sukuk is a transformative method of Crowdsourcing

I am thrilled that the Islamic Development Bank is taking the initiative to return Out-of-School Children and to use creative financial mechanism including the endowment concept as a noble Islamic based way to achieve this very bold goal. I think this is an ingenious idea and I want to get behind it, support it, and promote it. It is ingenious for many reasons. Third, this is a kind of modern crowdsourcing, because this is an invitation to the richest people who indeed should do more, but everybody also to put in a small amount, because this funding will tap to small contribution for those with modest means, as well a very large contribution for those of major means. At the end of 10-years those who have contributed can draw their funds or they can decide to make their funding as a permanent endowment. I believe that this is transformative in giving a long time perspective for development, in harnessing the endowment concept in Islamic finance for achieving sustainable development goal number 4, and I believe that this will mobilize huge interest and huge financing for phenomenal benefit for the children of the Islamic world. COMMUNITY PARTICIPATION

Community participation plays a key role in the implementation of development projects. Kalley-Plateau primary school in the outskirts of the city of Niamey in the Republic of Niger has a unique story. The community came together and constructed makeshift classes in a land provided by the government so that their children could have access to primary education. During the Workshop on Out-of-School Children organized by the Islamic Solidarity Fund for Development with other development partners in January 2020, the participants paid a historic visit to the school and engaged with the community to understand their challenges and initiate ways of working together to help the community achieve their dreams of providing quality education for their children. Saleh Idi is the leader of the community that established the school and he expressed his expectations on the Out-of-School Children initiative

My name is Saleh Idi and I am the President of Parents’ Association of Kalley-Plateau primary school and I am one of the Qur’anic teachers in the school. Historically there was no school in this area, and the community was growing. The parents come together every year to make contribution for the funding of the school. The government provide teachers while the community provide the structure and the equipment needed. It is a very good school and the community is working hard, but lack of equipment is a major challenge which affects the quality of education. For instance there is no furniture for the pupils to sit and study

At the third Gulf investors Gulf Debt Capital Market Summit in Dubai – UAE, Gulf investors welcomed the initiative of Islamic Development Bank (IsDB) to offer US$500 million Cash Waqf Sukuk (CWS). The Summit was organized by the Gulf Bond and Sukuk Association (GBSA) on 25 October 2019. Brs. Fawaz noted that the introduction of CWS is part of the innovative resource mobilization effort promoted by IsDB. The IsDB new business model championed by IsDB President Dr. Bandar Hajjar, emphasizes the need for innovative initiatives to maximize the impact of IsDB interventions in Member Countries.

Following the endorsement of CWS by the IsDB Shari’ah Board, the ISFD Board of Directors approved the issuance of CWS in February 2019 to the tune of US$500 million, with an initial issuance of up to US$200 million. As a resource mobilization tool, CWS targets all categories of benefactors (High-Net-Worth individuals as well as members of the public), to subscribe to the Sukuk. Proceeds will be invested, and the profit will be directed towards ISFD/IsDB poverty alleviation programs

COMMENTARY

SDG1: ACCELERATING POVERTY ALLEVIATION THROUGH CASH WAQF SUKUK

BY IBRAHIM FAWAZ ABDULNOUR

Officer-in-Charge, Crowd Engagement Funds Management Department,
Partnerships Development Complex

Reading some information about poverty alleviation, you might think that poverty is exclusively a problem of the Muslim World. although poverty is not an exclusive to the Muslim world, it is truly an alarming problem that should be treated as an emergency if SDG1, (No Poverty), is to be achieved by the year 2030

Despite the flourishing nature of Islamic finance, which has an estimated US$2 trillion in assets, some experts argue that it has not effectively succeeded in generating economic activities that would lift the poor out of poverty. As of 2019 there were an estimated 320 million in the OIC countries living on less than US$1.9 per day.

The un estimates the gap in financing to achieve the sdgs at us$2.5 trillion annually in developing countries alone (unctad, 2014)

While this is an issue of huge concern, it is also a great opportunity for the IsDB to deploy its influence to overcome the threat of poverty in the Muslim World by accelerating the drive for poverty alleviation through the introduction of Cash Waqf Sukuk (CWS). CWS is an innovative resource mobilizing tool, whereby the proceeds from Sukuk issuance will be directed to Shari’ah-compliant investments, and the net returns from these investments will be entirely forfeited by the Sukuk holders in favor of the ISFD for its programs and operations. However, upon maturity of the Sukuk, subscribers receive back their principal/subscription amount.

The Crowd Engagement and Funds Management Department (CEEFM) will lead the issuance of the Sukuk as part of a greater collaboration between IsDB’s Partnerships Development Complex and ISFD.

Sukuk holders are not motivated by financial return. Instead, they focus on the delivery of sustainable social outcomes and philanthropic goals. The CWS effectively uses of Temporary Cash Waqf (TCW) and Permanent Cash Waqf (PCW). It includes built-in option for Sukuk-holders to convert their TCW into a PCW on maturity if they wish. The Sharia Board of IsDB approved the CWS concept, and the Board of Directors of ISFD approved the issuance of US$500 CWS, with an initial issuance of US$200 million in 2020. The beauty of CWS is that it targets both High-Net-Worth-Individuals and members of the public who can subscribe for as little as US$100

It is important to stress that CWS is a “social” service, unlike the conventional Sukuk where subscribers receive a financial return. CWS aims to alleviate the suffering of the poor, and to create jobs or income generation opportunities. The CWS tenure is set at 10 years before the principal payment is paid to TCW Sukuk holders.

The UN estimates the gap in financing to achieve the SDGs at US$2.5 trillion annually in developing countries alone (UNCTAD, 2014).” Reliance on conventional sources of finance will not be sufficient to achieve these Goals. Islamic finance, through innovative resource mobilization tools like CWS can take center stage in any forum discussing the 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development.
